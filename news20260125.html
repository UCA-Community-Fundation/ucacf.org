UCACF Interview | Why did Rosemead take off in New York?

By UCACF • Janurary 25, 2026 • 5 min read

If Rosemead has been popping up in your New York Chinese community feed lately, it’s probably not by chance. Several screenings at AMC Lincoln Square sold out, and the film reportedly brought in about $50.2K from a single screen over its opening weekend. At the same time, it’s earned major recognition along the way—winning the Locarno Audience Award, taking Best Narrative Feature at Bentonville, and receiving a Golden Horse nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay. (Boston premiere details and ticket info are included at the end.)

This isn’t a movie that relies on spectacle or “feel-good” adrenaline—so how did it manage to pull audiences into theaters in its very first week?

To explore that question, I spoke online with the film’s executive producer, Ms. Jiang Fanghua. She’s not only an investor, but also someone who brought a group of first-time film backers into the project. From our conversation, one thing became clear: Rosemead isn’t simply being positioned as an “Asian American film.” It’s a project with bigger ambitions—one that begins in the community and hopes to reach a much wider American audience.

01 What does a “community film” really mean?

Before this interview, the phrase “community film” felt abstract to me—more like a cultural posture or an identity statement.

Ms. Jiang grounded it in something concrete. For her, “community” isn’t a slogan. It shows up in three practical ways: where the money comes from, who makes the film, and how audiences gather around it.

Q: Many people describe Rosemead as a “community film.” What does “community” mean to you here?

Ms. Jiang: “Community” can be small—you can say Chinese American. It can also be bigger—Asian American. Or even broader—American. But I don’t recommend naming specific countries or regions. There’s no need to turn it into a label.

She didn’t stop at definitions—she broke down what “community-based” looks like in practice.

First: funding from the community. Ms. Jiang shared that a meaningful portion of the film’s financing came from the Asian community, and many investors were backing a film for the first time. Some were motivated by trust in Lucy Liu; others simply believed the story mattered—that it deserved to be told.

To her, that “first-time” participation is significant. It isn’t commercial inertia. It’s a shared decision: these subject matters, and this story shouldn’t be ignored.

Second: a creative team rooted in the community. She emphasized that “community-based” isn’t only about funding. Many of the cast, lead creatives, and key production roles are Asian, which means the film’s portrayal isn’t built from an outsider’s imagination. It’s shaped by people who understand the texture and details of the lived experience.

That sensitivity, she said, “grows out of the community.” It’s why so many moments feel familiar and everyday—rather than falling into predictable “Asian storytelling” stereotypes.

Third: audiences begin in the community, but the film doesn’t stay there. Speaking about the New York previews and screenings, Ms. Jiang noted that Asian audiences were present—but so were many non-Asian viewers who chose to walk into the theater and engage.

For a film that begins with an Asian immigrant family, that matters. It suggests this isn’t a story meant only for “our own circle.”

Q: The film ultimately reaches a broader audience. How does it move beyond the limits of “community storytelling”?

Ms. Jiang said the story reflects something universal: human nature and challenges any family might face. It just happens to unfold within a Chinese immigrant household. Similar struggles exist across many communities.

“It’s like—we speak the truth first, and then we send the conversation into a larger space.”

That line stayed with me. Maybe this is the real meaning of a “community film”: community isn’t a boundary—it’s a starting point. Not a way to contain a story, but a way to launch it.

02 How is it different from the “traditional Hollywood” approach to Asian stories?

Q: If Hollywood also tells Asian stories, why does Rosemead still emphasize being “community-based,” even “community-led”?

Jiang: A lot of films that reach the mainstream begin with a community-based foundation. Rosemead also starts from the community—then reaches outward.

Then she shifted to what she sees as the key difference: how the story is told.

She said that Hollywood portrayals of Asian American stories can “miss the mark.” What makes Rosemead different is the combination of two strengths:

· a team with professional, Hollywood-level training; and

· genuine sensitivity to the everyday details of Asian community life. In her view, you need both.

She explained the “miss” with a simple contrast. Asian stories on screen often lean into familiar genres—kung fu action, or glossy spectacle like Crazy Rich Asians. Rosemead chooses something else: the day-to-day.

She also spoke about a reality many immigrants share: navigating the U.S. healthcare system can feel overwhelming. Language is one barrier; the system itself is another—what primary care is for, how referrals work, how to book specialists, how insurance functions. Immigrant communities often need both language support and practical guidance. And while this isn’t unique to Chinese families, mental health can be especially difficult to discuss openly in many Asian cultural contexts.

That’s why she believes one of the film’s strongest contributions is bringing mental health into public conversation—helping people understand its importance, possible causes, and what support exists. She also emphasized something crucial for community organizations: it’s not enough to say, “we have services.” People need to know where they are and how to access them—and that requires proactive outreach.

03 What the film keeps returning to: bringing mental health out of silence

In our conversation, Ms. Jiang returned to mental health. Her tone was restrained. The point wasn’t to dramatize tragedy—it was to ask whether we are willing to talk, and willing to seek help.

Q: What do you think Rosemead most wants to remind the community of?

Ms. Jiang: “It wants to generate conversation. The stigma around mental health is heavier in Asian families. First, shame—you don’t tell others. Second, distrust that medical approaches can help. Third, people often blame the individual—blame themselves or blame family. But often it’s not that someone ‘can’t handle it’ and then has problems; it’s that they already have problems, and that makes it even harder to cope.”

She offered a framing worth sitting with: we accept conditions like high blood pressure or diabetes and know to see a doctor—but when it becomes depression, anxiety, or serious mental illness, many people choose silence, even self-judgment.

She also stressed the role of community services:

Ms. Jiang: “A community can’t be satisfied with building services; it must ask how to deliver services. Some immigrants don’t even know help exists. They need support but don’t know where to go. We must be more proactive—reach out and let people know where services are.”

For anyone working in community spaces, that’s a sharp reminder: “having resources” is not the same as “people being able to access them.”

And one thing the film can do is break that silence—turn “shame” into something discussable and turn “not knowing” into something people can ask for help with.

04 Behind the scenes: the pandemic + the Hollywood strikes

Q: Is there a behind-the-scenes story you’d be willing to share—something most viewers wouldn’t know?

Ms. Jiang: Yes—and it changed how the film was made. The original plan was to shoot in California, but with the late-pandemic environment plus the writers’/actors’ strikes, scheduling constraints, and cost pressures, the team made major adjustments. They converted as many exterior scenes to interiors as possible, shifted much of the production to Queens, New York, and only returned to California for a small number of necessary exterior shots.

She described it calmly, but it landed with weight: the plan changed, the scenes changed, and the location changed. It reminded me that some films reaching the screen at all is already the result of enormous effort.

05 Why do people walk into the theater because of Lucy Liu?

Marketing always mentions stars, but I wanted to ask something more personal: what is Lucy Liu like when you work with her?

Q: What is Lucy like on set?

Ms. Jiang: “She’s one of the most hardworking people I’ve met—always on time, attention to detail, and very attentive to how people feel. She’s grounded. She also tells younger artists that passion matters most. If you lose passion, you shouldn’t stay in this industry, because filmmaking is truly hard.”

She said it plainly, without exaggeration—and that’s exactly why it felt credible.

What stayed with me wasn’t “how famous she is,” but the specific traits: punctual, meticulous, serious about the work, and mindful of others. These aren’t glamorous, but on a film set, they’re everything.

Ms. Jiang also mentioned that during the promotional run, Lucy was almost always traveling—because attention naturally centers on her. She didn’t frame it as hardship; she described it as the reality of leading from the front: if you’re the face of a project, you keep pushing it forward.

Yes, many people go to the theater because of Lucy Liu. But after hearing these details, I understand another layer: what you’re seeing isn’t only a familiar name—it’s someone who treats every part of the process with seriousness.

Which brings us back to the original question: why did Rosemead “sell out” in New York?

After the interview, I felt even more certain: community isn’t the film’s “label.” It’s its foundation. And that connects directly to UCACF’s mission as well—supporting community-led projects with small seed-grant resources so important issues can be seen, conversations can happen, and “resources exist” can genuinely become “resources are accessible,” with impact extending beyond the community into the wider public.
